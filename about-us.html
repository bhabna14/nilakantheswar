About Temple

Our faith is rooted in the worship of Lord Shiva

The Hindu god Shiva is also known as Nilakantha, which is a Sanskrit word that means "blue throat". This is because Shiva drank the poison kalakuta, which turned his throat blue. According to the Shruti-Smriti Puran, the devas and asuras churned the ocean to obtain Amruta, and one of the 14 ratnas they obtained was the poison kalakuta. This poison could destroy the entire world, but Shiva consumed it, turning his throat blue. To counteract the effects of the poison, Shiva meditated for 60,000 years under a Panchpani tree at the confluence of the Pankaja and Madhumati rivers. After his penance, he installed a shivaling in the shape of a throat beneath the tree.



The temple is named after Lord Nilakantheswar, a form of Lord Shiva. The name "Nilakantheswar" refers to the "Blue-Throated Lord," a title attributed to Shiva due to a famous legend from Hindu mythology. According to this legend, during the churning of the ocean (Samudra Manthan) by the gods (Devas) and demons (Asuras) to obtain the nectar of immortality (Amrita), a deadly poison called Halahala emerged from the ocean. To protect the universe from this poison, Lord Shiva swallowed it, holding it in his throat. The poison turned his throat blue, earning him the name "Nilakantha" (the blue-throated one).


The Sri Sri Nilakantheswar Temple is not just a place of worship but also a site of cultural and historical importance. It reflects the architectural style and religious practices of the region and serves as a center for various religious activities and festivals dedicated to Lord Shiva. Devotees flock to the temple, especially during major Shiva festivals like Mahashivaratri, to seek blessings and offer prayers to Lord Nilakantheswar.
